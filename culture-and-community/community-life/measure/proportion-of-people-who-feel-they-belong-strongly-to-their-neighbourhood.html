Sense of neighbourhood belonging

Location:
England
Time period:
2009/10 to 2010/11 and 2012/13 to 2015/16
Source:
Community Life Survey (previously Citizenship Survey)
Department:
Department of Culture, Media and Sport
Last updated:
20 July 2016

The main facts and figures show that:

  • in 2009/10, the survey found that 77% of White people felt strongly they belonged to their neighbourhood, compared to 72% of people from ethnic minorities

  • in 2015/16, the survey found that 72% of White people surveyed felt strongly that they belonged to their immediate neighbourhood, compared to 67% of people from ethnic minorities

  • survey results from 2015/16 should be treated with caution because the number of people surveyed (the ‘sample size’) was too small to draw firm conclusions

The reliability of the data

These figures are based on a England-only survey and are therefore estimates of the results for the total population.

Estimates based on a larger number of respondents are generally more reliable. Therefore, the smaller numbers of respondents from ethnic minority backgrounds mean that estimates for these groups are less reliable than estimates for White people.

For the same reason, observed changes over time may not reflect real differences, so caution is needed when interpreting short-term trends in the data.

Up until 2010/11, the data was collected through the Citizenship Survey, but from 2012/13 onwards it was collected through the Community Life Survey. The sample size was larger in the earlier years of the survey, making it easier to identify important differences between subgroups.

In 2016/17, the survey moved from face-to-face to online data collection. The sample size was increased and included a larger sample of ethnic minority respondents to produce more reliable results for this population. Therefore, more detailed and robust results should be available when the 2016/17 survey results are published at the end of July 2017.

What the data measures

The government’s Community Life Survey was established to monitor levels of community cohesion and engagement.

This data comes from a question which asked people how strongly they felt they belonged to their immediate neighbourhood.

Respondents who answered 'fairly strongly' or 'very strongly' to this question were judged to have a strong sense of belonging to their neighbourhood.

In 2011/12, the survey wasn’t run, so data for this year is missing.

Why the categories of ethnicities used in this data were chosen

This data is divided into 2 ethnic categories: White and Other, that is, White ethnic groups (including White British and White ethnic minorities) compared with all other ethnic minorities.

The small sample size for the Community Life Survey means that it's only possible to provide a breakdown for these two broad ethnic categories.

There may be differences between ethnic minorities, but it is not possible to explore this with the sample size currently available.

Purpose Of Data Source

The Community Life Survey is designed to track the latest trends and developments across areas that are key to encouraging social action and empowering communities, such as volunteering and charitable giving;

  • neighbourhood (views about the local area, community cohesion and belonging)

  • civic engagement

  • social action

  • well-being

Methodology

The Community Life Survey is a household survey of England carried out via face-to-face interviews.

The 2015-16 survey consisted of a sample size of 3,000 individuals although the sample size was larger in some earlier years. The results for this sample have been weighted to be representative of the population of England.

Data type

Survey

Disclosure control

We do not publish results based on fewer than 30 respondents. All the results presented here are based on sample sizes of over 200 respondents
