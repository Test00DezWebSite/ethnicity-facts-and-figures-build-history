Judges and non-legal members of courts and tribunals in the workforce

Department:
Ministry of Justice
Last updated:
04 August 2017, see all updates
Source:
Judicial Statistics
Area covered:
United Kingdom
Time period:
April 2015 and April 2016

The main facts and figures show that:

  • 6% of judges in courts who did state their ethnicity in April 2016 were from an ethnic minority group

  • observations on ethnicity of judges in courts based on this data should be treated with caution because of the large number of judges who didn’t state their ethnicity

  • as at April 2016, the ethnicity of about 16% of judges in courts, and 7% of tribunal judges is not known

  • 14% of tribunal judges and non-legal members of the judiciary who did state their ethnicity in April 2016 were from an ethnic minority group, roughly the same as one year earlier

Things you need to know

Judges and non-legal members of the judiciary are asked to record their ethnicity on a voluntary basis, and not all do so.

This adds a degree of uncertainty to the breakdowns by ethnicity. For judges in court, this uncertainty is relatively high because ethnicity is unknown for 16% of them. For tribunal judges and non-legal members of the judiciary there is a lesser degree of uncertainty with the breakdown because of the higher number reporting their ethnicity.

The source of the data is a new electronic human resources system for the judiciary that contains data migrated from a number of existing systems, some of which has yet to be validated.

What the data measures

The data breaks down the number of judges in courts in England and Wales by ethnicity. It does the same for judges and non-legal members of the judiciary in tribunals in England and Wales and in employment tribunals in Scotland.

The data includes judges and non-legal members of the judiciary in tribunals administered by HM Courts & Tribunals Service (HMCTS) as well as those in Wales that aren’t administered by HMCTS.

Why these ethnic categories were chosen

Judges are asked to place themselves within the following 5 broad ethnic categories:

  • White

  • Mixed/Multiple ethnic groups

  • Asian / Asian British/Chinese

  • Black/African/Caribbean/Black British

  • Other ethnic group

Summary

This data shows that:

  • the proportion of judges and members of tribunals from an ethnic minority is twice that of judges in courts, where ethnicity is known

  • the largest ethnic minority group is Asian; this group make up 6.3% of judges in courts and tribunal members combined, where the ethnicity is known
