Rates of criminal reoffending

Department:
Ministry of Justice
Last updated:
04 August 2017, see all updates
Source:
NOMS Offender Equalities Statistics Bulletin
Area covered:
England and Wales
Time period:
2000 to 2015

The main facts and figures show that:

  • Black offenders consistently had the highest rates of reoffending between 2004 and 2014

  • offenders in the Other ethnic group had the lowest rate

  • across all ethnic groups, under-18s had the highest rate of reoffending – and their rate of reoffending increased between 2010 and 2015

  • the North East of England had the highest rate of reoffending in 2015

  • use caution when considering these figures and avoid direct comparisons based on them, as they don’t account for important factors such as the nature of the crimes or the number of previous crimes

Things you need to know

There are 2 time series for this data: one for the calendar years 2004 to 2014, which gives rates for reoffending broken down by ethnicity; another for each year ending June 2000 to 2015, which gives rates of reoffending broken down by ethnicity, age and geographical area. The total figures for reoffending for a given year will not agree between the different times series because one uses calendar years and the other years ending in June. This is not an error.

Data isn’t available for 2001 because of a problem with archived data on Court Orders.

Measuring reoffending is difficult. Official records are taken from either the police or courts, but they underestimate the true level of reoffending.

This is because not all crimes are detected and not all crimes and sanctions are recorded on one central system.

Other methods of measuring reoffending, such as self-report studies, are likely to also underestimate the rate.

Percentage changes may not add up to 100, due to the rounding of raw figures.

What the data measures

This data measures the percentage of criminal offenders who reoffend (that is, the reoffending rate). This is broken down by ethnicity for each calendar year from 2004 to 2014. The data is additionally broken down by ethnicity, age and geographical area for each year ending June from 2000 to 2015.

Reoffences are only counted if they:

  • are committed within one year of the offender’s previous offence (or 18 months if the case is still progressing through the courts after a year)
  • lead to a court conviction, caution, reprimand or warning
Why these ethnic categories were chosen

For this data, the number of people counted was too small to draw any firm conclusions about detailed ethnic categories. Therefore, the data is broken down into the following broad groups:

  • White
  • Black
  • Asian
  • Other ethnic group

Methodology and type of data

Type of data

Administrative data

Purpose of data source

Admin data used for internal management information

Methodology

The Lammy review calculates a relative rate index from the Proportion of Offenders who Reoffend. To calculate this, divide the proportion for the ethnic group of interest by the proportion for the White group.

A certain proportion of offenders who could not be matched to the Police National Computer (PNC) are excluded from the offender cohort. Therefore, these numbers do not represent all proven offenders. Admin data sources reported as recorded. As with all large administrative data sources, some inherent inaccuracy may exist.

Rounding

Rates as a percentage to 1 decimal place

Suppression rules

Proportions and averages based on less than 30 offenders are removed as they make data unreliable for interpretation. Number of reoffenders, reoffences and previous offences based on less than five offenders are suppressed. This is to prevent the disclosure of individual information.
