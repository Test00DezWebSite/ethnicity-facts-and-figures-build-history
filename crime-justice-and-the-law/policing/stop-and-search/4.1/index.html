1. Main facts and figures

  • between April 2018 and March 2019, there were 375,588 stop and searches in England and Wales (excluding vehicle searches)
  • there were 7 stop and searches for every 1,000 people, down from 25 per 1,000 people between April 2009 and March 2010
  • stop and search rates have fallen for every ethnic group, but at different rates
  • between April 2018 and March 2019, there were 4 stop and searches for every 1,000 White people, compared with 38 for every 1,000 Black people
  • almost half of all stop and searches took place in the Metropolitan Police force area in London
  • there were 22 stop and searches for every 1,000 people in London, the highest rate out of all police force areas
  • London had the highest stop and search rates for all ethnic groups apart from Black (Dorset) and White (Merseyside)
Things you need to know

Stop and search rates are given to the nearest whole number. But all comparisons and differences have been worked out using unrounded data.

From 2017/18, stop and search figures have included stops under section 47a of the Terrorism Act. All figures in this data reflect this. There were no stop and searches under the Terrorism Act between 2011/12 and 2016/17, nor in 2018/19.

Someone who is stopped and searched is usually asked for their ethnicity. The circumstances of a stop and search may affect the accuracy of this information.

This can be seen by the high rate of stop and searches for the â€˜Otherâ€™ categories within most ethnic groups. For example:

  • 37% of stop and searches involving Asian people in 2018/19 were assigned to the â€˜Other Asianâ€™ ethnic group
  • 40% of those involving Black people were assigned to the 'Other Black' ethnic group

If someone doesnâ€™t give their ethnicity, itâ€™s recorded as â€˜unreportedâ€™. These stop and searches are included in the rate for â€˜Allâ€™ ethnic groups.

The percentage of stop and searches recorded as having an â€˜unreportedâ€™ ethnicity is:

  • 2018/19 â€“ 15%
  • 2017/18 â€“ 11%
  • 2016/17 â€“ 10%
  • 2015/16 â€“ 9%
  • 2014/15 â€“ 6%
  • 2013/14 â€“ 5%
  • 2012/13 â€“ 5%
  • 2011/12 â€“ 4%
  • 2010/11 â€“ 4%
  • 2009/10 â€“ 5%

Vehicle searches are excluded from this data as ethnicity information is not collected during the search.

Data analysed by police force area

Download the data to see the number of stop and searches by ethnicity and police force area.

Population estimates are used to work out the rate per 1,000 people in each area.

Treat the police force area figures as estimates because:

  • someone stopped and searched in a particular area may not live in that area
  • population estimates for each area only include permanent residents, not visitors
  • population estimates use Census figures from 2011 and may have changed since then
  • the ethnic makeup of different areas, including the ethnicity of visitors, may affect figures

National rates

Treat national stop and search rates with caution. This is because stop and searches are not spread evenly across England and Wales.

National rates are influenced by the higher number of incidents involving certain ethnic groups in certain police force areas. For example, the Metropolitan Police made 79% of all searches of Black people in 2018 to 2019.

National rates may not reflect the rates in any police force area, only all police force areas on average.

What the data measures

The data shows the stop and search rates for different ethnic groups in England and Wales.

Figures include a rate per 1,000 people for each ethnic group and area. This rate is worked out by dividing the number of stop and searches with the population for that group.

The police can stop and search someone they think is doing something illegal, under:

  • section 1 of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (and associated legislation including section 47 of the Firearms Act 1968 and section 23 of the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971)
  • section 60 of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994
  • section 44/47A of the Terrorism Act 2000
The ethnic categories used in this data

Stop and search data uses the ethnic categories from the 2001 Census.

Population estimates come from the 2011 Census. They have been adjusted so that the Chinese ethnic group is included in the â€˜Otherâ€™ ethnic group instead of the â€˜Asianâ€™ ethnic group (as in the 2001 Census).

Summary of Stop and search By ethnicity over time Summary

This data shows that, between 2017/18 and 2018/19:

  • the national rate increased for the first time since 2009/10, from 5 to 7 stop and searches per 1,000 people
  • stop and search rates increased in every ethnic group except White British and Chinese (where they stayed the same)

Between 2009/10 and 2018/19:

  • the national stop and search rate went down from 25 to 7 per 1,000 people
  • the stop and search rate for White people was lower than the national rate every year

  • the rates for the Asian, Black, and Mixed ethnic groups were higher than the national rate every year

  • the Chinese, White British, and Mixed White and Asian ethnic groups consistently had the lowest stop and search rates out of all 16 ethnic groups

  • the Black African, Black Caribbean and Other Black groups consistently had the highest rates

Summary of Stop and search By ethnicity and area Summary

This data shows that, between April 2018 and March 2019:

  • the Metropolitan Police in London made almost half (48%) of all stops and searches in England and Wales
  • there were 22 stop and searches for every 1,000 people in London, compared with 4 per 1,000 people in the rest of England and Wales
  • there were 51 stop and searches for every 1,000 Black people in London, compared with 19 per 1,000 Black people in the rest of England and Wales
  • London had the highest rates of stop and search for all ethnic groups apart from Black (Dorset) and White (Merseyside)
  • police in Dorset were 25 times more likely to stop Black people than White people (the biggest difference between ethnic groups)
  • police were 14 times more likely to stop Black people than White people in Sussex and West Mercia
  • Black people had the highest stop and search rates in every police force area for which there was data
  • the rate for Asian people was 5 times higher than the rate for White people in Gwent
  • the rate for people with Mixed ethnicity was 5 times the rate for White people in Gloucestershire
  • Gloucestershire, Cleveland and Greater Manchester had the lowest overall rates

Summary of Stop and search By ethnicity and legislation over time Summary

This data covers stop and search of individuals under the following acts:

  • section 1 of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (and associated legislation including section 23 of the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971) â€“ the police can stop and search someone they think is carrying items like stolen property or drugs
  • section 44/47a of the Terrorism Act 2000 â€“ the police can stop and search someone if they suspect an act of terrorism is about to take place (after authorisation by a senior officer)
  • section 60 of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994 â€“ the police can stop and search someone within an authorised area to prevent violence involving weapons

This data shows that, between April 2018 and March 2019:

  • 97% of all stop and searches in England and Wales were under section 1 of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act
  • 3% of stop and searches were under section 60 of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act, up from 0.2% in the financial year ending March 2018
  • no stop and searches were conducted under section 47A of the Terrorism Act
  • there were 13,083 stop and searches under section 60, up from 628 in 2016/17 and 2,496 in 2017/18
  • 4,858 section 60 stop and searches involved Black people, the highest number out of all ethnic groups for this legislation
  • 1,827 section 60 stop and searches involved Asian people, compared with 272 the previous year (the biggest increase out of all ethnic groups)

6. Methodology

The 43 territorial police forces in England and Wales provide stop and search data to the Home Office. The British Transport Police also provides data.

Police officers have to record the details of a stop and search when it happens. The person they stop and search can ask for a copy of the record. This reduces the risk of a stop and search going unrecorded.

Some police forces record stop and searches electronically. Others use paper records, which are more likely to involve errors, or delays in uploading.

HM Inspectorate of Constabulary and Fire & Rescue Services (HMICFRS) monitors stop and search. It carries out regular inspections and produces reports on its inspections.

The Home Office carries out quality assurance checks on the data. Police forces have to explain any unusual trends, or resubmit any inconsistent data. All data is then confirmed by police forces before itâ€™s published.

Population estimates come from the 2011 Census but stop and search data uses the ethnic categories from the 2001 Census. The population estimates are adjusted so that the Chinese ethnic group is included in the â€˜Otherâ€™ ethnic group instead of the â€˜Asianâ€™ ethnic group.

Rounding

Stop and search rates are given to the nearest whole number. You can see more detailed estimates if you download the data.

Purpose of data source

Figures on stop and search incidents resulting in arrest create greater transparency in the use of stop and search in England and Wales. They enhance accountability by enabling the public to monitor police forces at a national and local level.

The data is used to form a national picture of the trends in stop and search, and resulting arrests. It informs discussions about crime, policing and criminal justice in government and academia, and ensures the public are accurately informed.
