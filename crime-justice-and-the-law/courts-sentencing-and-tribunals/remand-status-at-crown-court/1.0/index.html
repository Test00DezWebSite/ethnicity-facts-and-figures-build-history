1. Main facts and figures

  • in 2016, the percentage of defendants at the Crown Court remanded in custody fell for most ethnic groups compared to the years from 2012 to 2015

  • in 2016, Black defendants were more likely than any other ethnic group to be remanded in custody

  • from 2012 to 2016, White defendants were consistently less likely to be remanded in custody, and consistently more likely to have a remand status of â€˜not remandedâ€™, than any other ethnic group

Things you need to know

The Ministry of Justice's case management system (CREST) only gives data for remand in the Crown Court. Magistrates' courts are not included because there is not enough ethnicity data for offences dealt with by those courts. This also means that defendants who originally appeared in a magistrates' court, and later appeared in the Crown Court, will not be counted twice.

If a defendant has more than one remand status during court proceedings, only the most serious status will be recorded. For example, if a defendant is remanded on bail and then later remanded in custody, their status will be recorded as custody.

The data does not include details of the offence for which the defendant has been charged. It is likely that the severity of the offence will influence the type of remand status (bail or custody) given to the defendant.

What the data measures

This data measures the percentage of defendants in the Crown Court, within different ethnic groups, who are remanded on bail or custody, or not remanded.

Remand is the way that people who are charged with a crime (â€˜defendantsâ€™) are dealt with until the court hearings for that crime are completed. They can be:

  • remanded on bail, meaning they are allowed to return home until the court hearing (sometimes with certain conditions)
  • remanded in custody, which means they will be held in prison
  • not remanded, meaning they have not been given bail or custody at some point during the proceedings

After arrest, the police will decide a defendantâ€™s remand status (bail or custody), which lasts until their first court hearing at the magistratesâ€™ court. After that, the courts will decide the defendantâ€™s remand status at each hearing in the proceedings. Different remand decisions for the same defendant may be made during the court process.

Some offences are dealt with entirely in the magistratesâ€™ court, but others (usually more serious offences) go from there to the Crown Court. This data focuses on defendants in the Crown Court.

Defendants are also classified by age group, so that:

  • adults are aged 21 and over
  • young adults are aged 18 to 20
  • juveniles are under the age of 18

The data is taken from the Ministry of Justice's case management system for the Crown Court (CREST).

The ethnic categories used in this data

The analysis uses the following broad ethnic categories, based on the 2001 Census:

  • White
  • Mixed / Multiple ethnic groups
  • Asian / Asian British
  • Black / African / Caribbean / Black British
  • Other ethnic group (which includes Chinese)

There is also a â€˜not statedâ€™ category, which is made up of people whose ethnicity has not been recorded or who chose not to state their ethnicity.

5. Methodology

The analysis uses administrative data from the Crown Court (CREST). The data counts individual people (defendants) not cases. Multiple defendants are counted for one case where applicable. Each defendant is only counted once, even if multiple offences are committed. When a defendant has been found guilty of two or more offences it is the offence for which the heaviest penalty is imposed.

Every effort is made to ensure that the figures presented are accurate and complete. However, it is important to note that this data has been extracted from large administrative data systems generated by the courts and police forces. As a consequence, care should be taken to ensure data collection processes and their inevitable limitations are taken into account when this data is used.

Rounding

Data is rounded to 1 decimal place
