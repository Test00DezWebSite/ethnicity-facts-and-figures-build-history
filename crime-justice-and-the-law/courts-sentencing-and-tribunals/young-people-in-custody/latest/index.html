Young people in custody

Department:
Ministry of Justice
Published:
22 December 2017
Last updated:
02 March 2018, see all updates
Source:
Youth justice statistics
Area covered:
England and Wales
Time period:
2005/06 to 2015/16

The main facts and figures show that:

  • from 2005/06 to 2015/16, the number of young people in custody went down for all ethnic groups

  • in the same period, the percentage of the youth custody population made up of young people from Asian and Other backgrounds doubled, and the percentage from Black backgrounds increased

  • most young people held in custody in 2015/16 were held for violence against the person offences (34%)

Things you need to know

If a young person has more than one custodial order at the same time, the data is based on the most serious custodial order.

The data is from the Youth Justice Board database, which includes young people in:

  • young offender institutions
  • secure children’s homes
  • secure training centres

The Youth Justice Board changed its database and the way it recorded some data in March 2012. Until then, most breaches of a statutory order were recorded as a separate type of offence. This changed after March 2012, and this is believed to account for a large decrease in the percentage of young people sentenced for breaching a statutory order from 2012/13 onward.

This data does not include young people in police custody.

What the data measures

The data measures the average number of young people aged 10 to 17 years in custody in the time period covered. The data is broken down by ethnicity, type of offence (‘offence group’), and type of custodial order (‘legal basis for detention’).

Because the number of young people in custody varies from month to month, an average is worked out for each 12-month period (1 April to 31 March). The number of young people in custody is counted on a specific day every month, then an average is then worked out based on 12 months’ worth of counts.

The statistics on this page categorise custodial orders as one of 4 types:

  • a section 91 order is used for custodial sentences of more than 24 months for serious crimes (except murder), and can only be passed by a Crown Court
  • a Detention and Training Order (DTO) is 4 to 24 months long and includes both custody and training - it can only be used for young people aged 12 to 17 years
  • remand is when the young person is put into youth detention accommodation when they have been charged with an offence and are detained until a trial or sentencing hearing
  • other sentences, which include detention for public protection (‘section 226’), extended sentences (‘section 226B’) and life sentence for murder with a minimum period in custody (‘section 90’)

This data includes an analysis by 6 types of offence, plus ‘Other offences’. Types of offences under ‘Other offences’ include:

  • arson
  • breach of bail
  • criminal damage
  • death or injury by dangerous driving
  • fraud and forgery
  • motoring offences
  • non-domestic burglary
  • public order offence
  • theft and handling stolen goods
  • vehicle theft or unauthorised taking
The ethnic categories used in this data

For this data, the youth custody population was too small to draw any firm conclusions about specific ethnic categories. Therefore, the data is broken down into the following 5 broad groups:

  • Asian/Asian British
  • Black/African/Caribbean/Black British
  • Mixed/Multiple ethnic groups
  • White
  • Other ethnic group

For the data covering types of crime and types of custodial order, the following 2 categories have been used:

  • White (including White British and White ethnic minorities)
  • Other (all other ethnic minorities)

Summary

This data shows that:

  • in 2015/16, the average number of young people in custody was 959, about one-third the number in 2005/06 (2,831)

  • from 2005/06 to 2015/16, the number of young people in custody went down for all ethnic groups - the biggest decrease was in White young people, from 2,031 in 2005/06 to 556 in 2015/16

  • in 2015/16, White young people made up the highest percentage of young people in custody (at 58.0%); but this has decreased by over 13 percentage points since 2005/06, when 71.7% of young people in custody were White

  • from 2005/06 to 2015/16, the percentage of the youth custody population from Asian and Other backgrounds doubled, from 5.2% in 2005/06 to 10.4% in 2015/16; the percentage from Black backgrounds also increased, from 12.5% in 2005/06 to to 21.3% in 2015/16

Summary

This data shows that:

  • in 2015/16, 58.2% of White young people in custody were held on a Detention and Training Order (DTO), 19.1% were on remand, 19.0% had a section 91 order, and 3.8% had another type of custodial order

  • in the same period, 43.5% of young people from all other ethnic groups combined were held on a Detention and Training Order (DTO), 26.1% were on remand, 24.9% had a section 91 order, and 5.5% had another type of custodial order

  • in 2015/16 a higher percentage of young people from both groups were given a section 91 order compared with 5 years ago – 19.0% of White young people and 24.9% of young people from all other ethnic groups combined were given that type of order (compared with 12.9% and 19.0% respectively in 2010/11)

  • in 2015/16, young people from the Other ethnic group were less likely to be put on remand than they were 5 years ago – 26.1% were put on remand in 2015/16 compared with 32.6% in 2010/11

Summary

This data shows that:

  • in 2015/16, 29.8% of White young people and 39.8% of young people from all other ethnic groups combined were held in youth custody for violence against the person offences – higher than for any other type of offence

  • in the 5 years from 2010/11 to 2015/16, the percentage of White young people convicted for robbery increased (from 18.4% to 23.3%), while the percentage of young people from all other ethnic groups combined convicted for the same offence decreased (from 31.3% to 26.9%)

  • in 2015/16, a lower percentage of White young people were held in custody for drugs offences compared with young people from all other ethnic groups combined (at 3.9% and 12.6% respectively), while a higher percentage of White young people were held for sexual offences (at 12.1% of White young people and 6.2% of those from other all ethnic groups combined)

Methodology

Methodology

Young people self-report their ethnicity. Staff working in secure training centres and young offender institutions then record this information on an administrative system.

The numbers of young people in custody are counted at the beginning of each month, updated to include new admissions during the month, and then averaged across a 12-month period.

Rounding

Percentages have been rounded to 1 decimal place.

Purpose of data source

Youth justice data is used by the government to develop, monitor and evaluate criminal justice policy for young offenders. It reports on activity in the criminal justice system for England and Wales, giving information for the latest year and also longer-term trends.
